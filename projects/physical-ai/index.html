Portfolio case study · Physical AI data operations

Physical AI Data Pipeline

Project Manager · September 2025 – February 2026 · Abby (Dahm Won) Heo

Researchers said review was impossible. I diagnosed the missing review structure, defined the Task/Subtask operating model and staged review workflow, and helped turn Physical AI data collection into training-readiness decisions.

Physical AI refers to AI systems that perceive, decide, and act in the physical world, such as robots and autonomous vehicles. This case study focuses on robot manipulation data, such as robotic-arm tasks where a robot picks up, moves, or places objects, and the review workflow needed to make that data ready for model training.

Situation

The Ministry of Science and ICT’s Physical AI Leading Technology Development Program was a KRW 34 billion national program to develop a Physical AI foundation model. A consortium centered on the lead research company was formed to compete for the program against other consortiums. The lead research company led model development, while my company joined because of its experience running large-scale data collection and processing projects. It seconded me and one engineer to the lead research company to support the data-pipeline work from inside the model-development team.

That experience mattered, but this was not a routine data-collection project. The lead research company had experience collecting autonomous-driving data, but robot manipulation data for Physical AI had very little domestic precedent. My company also had data-pipeline experience, but no ready-made Physical AI playbook. Both organizations were defining the operating model while the government proposal and the project structure were still being shaped.

In that embedded role, I helped define how Physical AI data should be collected, reviewed, and prepared for training. The goal was not simply to increase data volume, but to make sure the researchers’ model-development work could be supported by usable training data.

Diagnosis

In the initial meeting, the lead research company’s researchers described quality review as the immediate blocker. Sensor-based auto-QC was already in place, but it did not solve the training-data decision problem. It could flag potential anomalies, but it could not determine whether a flagged case was a true outlier or whether unflagged Physical AI data was actually usable for model training. Those judgments still required human review of the video, task outcome, and failure context.

The visible demand was more data: more episodes, more scenarios, and faster collection. I diagnosed a different bottleneck. The project did not yet have an operational review layer that could turn collected Physical AI data into training-ready data. If collection scaled before that layer existed, the team would accumulate more data without knowing which parts improved the model and which parts degraded it.

The underlying issue was data granularity. Robot manipulation tasks needed to be broken into reviewable subtasks, because a failed episode was not always wholly useless and a completed episode was not always training-ready. Without defined review units, decision criteria, and escalation ownership, the team could not consistently decide what to keep, discard, recollect, or escalate.

Approach

Task/Subtask structure

To turn the diagnosis into an operating structure, I first researched how other companies structured similar robot data-collection workflows. The academic and industry terminology was not yet standardized; different companies used different labels, but the underlying intent was similar: break a full robot run into smaller units that could be collected, reviewed, stored, and analyzed more consistently.

I summarized that research internally and proposed Task/Subtask as our working vocabulary. I then used the same framing in interviews with the lead research company’s Head of Research to map the review problem at the task and subtask level. Treating an entire episode as pass or fail made it hard to identify which part of the robot task failed, which failure patterns were repeating, or how much action data of each type existed in the database.

Under this structure, each episode remained the task-level collection unit, but it could be broken into subtasks for collection, review, and storage. That changed three things: workers could receive subtask-level demonstration guides; reviewers could pinpoint failed subtasks and separate usable spans from unusable ones; and the data layer could track action-level coverage instead of treating each episode as one undifferentiated record.

Field benchmarking in China

A second question was whether non-specialist Initial Reviewers should be trained to read sensor graphs. I wanted to validate that assumption before designing the review process around it. The sensor values that matter differ by task, and their meaning depends on the collection objective. Asking general reviewers to judge graph movements by eye would not scale. Sensor-based auto-QC was the right layer for identifying candidate anomalies; human review needed to focus on video evidence, task outcome, and escalation.

To benchmark a more mature operating model, I researched Chinese humanoid robotics companies and helped arrange meetings without relying on domestic competitors that were unlikely to share internal practices. During the trip, we visited a robotics operator’s data-collection floor in China and observed how review work was divided in practice.

The pattern was clear. Reviewers primarily watched video. Sensor graphs were handled by auto-QC rather than by frontline reviewers. Back home, I shared meeting notes and floor sketches, giving the team concrete evidence for a video-centered first-pass review model.

Workflow redesign

Based on the research and field benchmarking, I redesigned the review process as a staged workflow instead of a single pass/fail review. The workflow separated first-pass video review, PM-level review for ambiguous cases, and expert review for the hardest training-usability decisions.

The key was to keep sensor-based auto-QC and human review in different roles. Auto-QC identified candidate anomaly windows; human reviewers checked those signals against video evidence, task outcome, and escalation context before a training-readiness decision was made. Each stage preserved the prior decision and evidence trail, so later reviewers did not start from zero.

I reviewed and refined the platform mock-up with the engineer seconded from my company and used it to brief the workflow to the lead research company’s business lead.

What Changed

By the end of my secondment, I could confirm two concrete operating changes.

First, the Task/Sub-Task structure moved beyond a planning document. It was reflected in the demo version I worked on with the engineer seconded from my company, and I later confirmed that the same structure appeared in the launched platform shown on my company’s homepage. The Task/Sub-Task structure gave the team a shared way to break robot runs into reviewable units and track action-level coverage, instead of treating each episode as a single pass/fail record.

Second, the review workflow changed. The platform design moved away from asking non-specialist reviewers to interpret sensor graphs and toward video-centered first-pass review with escalation. Sensor-based auto-QC remained the layer for identifying candidate anomaly windows; human review focused on task outcome, video evidence, and training-readiness judgment. Ambiguous cases could move to PM-level review or expert review.

At the program level, the consortium proposal was selected for the KRW 34 billion national program. Of the two companies responsible for the data pipeline, my company was the only one embedded directly with the lead research company’s model-development team.

Framing

For me, the Deployment Strategist signal in this project is the judgment behind the structure. The researchers had a real review problem, but the complaint was too broad to act on. The team needed a way to define review units, assign ownership for ambiguous cases, and record training-readiness decisions consistently.

My part was to make that structure concrete. I researched comparable robot data-collection practices, used the Task/Sub-Task framing in interviews with the lead research company, and checked the review model through field benchmarking in China. That work led to a video-centered first-pass review workflow, with sensor-based auto-QC used to flag candidate anomaly windows.

I then worked with the engineer seconded from my company to reflect those requirements in the demo and briefed the workflow to the lead research company’s business lead.

The structure did not stay abstract. It shaped the demo version I worked on, and I later confirmed that the same Task/Sub-Task structure appeared in the launched platform shown on my company’s public website.

This is the kind of work I want to do as a Deployment Strategist: turn unclear operating problems into workflows people can actually use.
